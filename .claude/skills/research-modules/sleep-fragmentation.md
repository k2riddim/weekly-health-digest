# Sleep Fragmentation — Research Module

## How cohabitant wake-ups propagate to recovery

Infant cohabitants create a distinct sleep-disruption pattern: multiple forced awakenings per night, often at unpredictable intervals. Unlike self-initiated waking (e.g., insomnia), these are external and non-negotiable. The physiological impact depends on: number of wake-ups, duration of each, time spent in light vs deep sleep before interruption, and total sleep time achieved.

## Impact on HRV and RHR

Sleep fragmentation reduces the proportion of deep (N3) and REM sleep, which are the primary windows for parasympathetic dominance. This suppresses overnight HRV recovery and can elevate resting heart rate by 3–8 bpm. The effect is cumulative: 3+ nights of fragmented sleep produce a measurable HRV downtrend that mimics overtraining, even if training load is unchanged.

## Next-day readiness

Fragmented sleep impairs reaction time, perceived exertion calibration (RPE inflation), and glycogen resynthesis. An athlete sleeping 7h in fragments may have the recovery profile of 5–5.5h of consolidated sleep. This means training load calculations based on time and HR alone can underestimate the true physiological cost.

## Distinguishing from training-induced fatigue

The key differentiator: sleep-fragmentation-driven HRV drops occur on rest days too, while training-induced drops correlate with session timing. Cross-reference HRV with baby MCP sleep data (night wakings count and timing) to attribute causality. If HRV suppression tracks cohabitant wake-up nights more closely than training days, sleep fragmentation is the dominant driver.
