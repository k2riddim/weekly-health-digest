# Labs — Optimal Ranges for Endurance Athletes — Research Module

## Where sports-medicine ranges diverge from lab references

Standard laboratory reference ranges represent 95% confidence intervals for the general population, which includes sedentary individuals. For endurance athletes, several biomarkers have different optimal targets.

## Key markers

**Ferritin**: Lab normal is often 12–300 ng/mL. Sports medicine consensus targets >50 ng/mL for endurance athletes, with some practitioners targeting >80 ng/mL. Below 30 ng/mL, iron supplementation is typically recommended regardless of hemoglobin status, as depleted stores impair oxygen transport capacity before frank anemia develops.

**Vitamin D (25-OH)**: Lab normal ≥20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L). Athletic performance literature suggests optimal range of 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L). Deficiency is common in northern latitudes and indoor trainers. Supplementation of 2000–4000 IU/day is standard in deficient athletes.

**Testosterone (total, free)**: Normal ranges are wide (300–1000 ng/dL total). Overtraining, under-fueling, and chronic sleep deprivation can suppress levels. Downward trends within the normal range are more informative than absolute values. Cross-reference with training load, sleep, and body composition trends.

**Inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6)**: Mild transient elevation after hard training is normal. Persistent elevation (hsCRP >3 mg/L) without recent intense exercise warrants investigation. Cross-reference with illness symptoms, sleep quality, and training monotony.
