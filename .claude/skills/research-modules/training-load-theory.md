# Training Load Theory — Research Module

## ACWR (Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio)

The ACWR compares recent load (acute, typically 7-day) to longer-term load (chronic, typically 28-day rolling average). The "sweet spot" of 0.8–1.3 is associated with lower injury risk (Gabbett 2016). Below 0.8 suggests detraining; above 1.3 indicates a spike that elevates injury risk. The ratio is most useful as a guardrail against sudden ramp-ups, not as a precise predictor.

## Banister impulse-response model

The fitness-fatigue model (Banister 1991) posits that training produces both a positive fitness effect (slow decay, ~45 days) and a negative fatigue effect (fast decay, ~15 days). Performance is the difference. This means acute fatigue masks fitness gains — a taper reveals accumulated fitness. Practical implication: chronic load reflects fitness investment; acute load reflects current fatigue.

## Foster monotony and strain

Training monotony = weekly mean load / SD of daily loads. High monotony (>2.0) with high strain (monotony × weekly load) is associated with illness and overtraining. Varying session intensity and duration across the week keeps monotony low. The 80/20 rule (80% low intensity, 20% high intensity) naturally produces good variability.

## Chronic load as fitness proxy

The 28-day rolling average of daily training load serves as a proxy for fitness status. Comparing current chronic load to historical peak chronic load gives "distance from peak" — a useful framing for rebuild trajectories. Safe rebuild targets ~10% weekly ramp in acute load, with chronic load lagging by design.
