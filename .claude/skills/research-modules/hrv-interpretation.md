# HRV Interpretation — Research Module

## Physiology overview

Heart rate variability reflects autonomic nervous system balance between sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) branches. In endurance athletes, higher resting HRV generally indicates good parasympathetic tone and cardiovascular fitness. The most commonly tracked metric is rMSSD (root mean square of successive R-R interval differences), which predominantly reflects parasympathetic modulation.

## Endurance-athlete baselines

Trained endurance athletes typically show rMSSD values of 60–120 ms, substantially above population norms (20–40 ms). Individual baselines matter far more than population averages — a 90-day rolling mean establishes the personal norm. Deviations >1.5σ below the personal mean for ≥3 consecutive days warrant investigation. A single low day is noise; a trend is signal.

## Key confounders

HRV is sensitive to many non-training factors: alcohol (suppresses for 24–48h), late meals, sleep quality (especially deep sleep percentage), hydration status, ambient temperature, illness onset (often visible in HRV 24–48h before symptoms), and psychological stress. Cohabitant-driven sleep fragmentation (night wakings) can suppress HRV without implying overtraining.

## Distinguishing poor recovery from autonomic shifts

A sustained HRV drop paired with elevated RHR and poor sleep scores suggests genuine recovery deficit. A sustained HRV drop with *normal* RHR and good sleep may indicate a beneficial autonomic adaptation (e.g., increased training load being absorbed). Cross-reference with training load trends, subjective readiness scores, and skin temperature to disambiguate.
